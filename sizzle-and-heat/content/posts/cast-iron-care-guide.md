Cast iron is one of the most durable, versatile pieces of cookware you can own — some skillets get passed down for generations. But it only earns that reputation when it's cared for properly. Get it wrong, and you'll end up with rust, sticking, and a pan that seems more trouble than it's worth.

This guide walks through everything you need to know: seasoning, everyday cleaning, storage, and the mistakes that ruin cast iron fastest.

## What "Seasoning" Actually Means

Seasoning is a layer of polymerized oil baked onto the surface of the iron. It's what gives well-cared-for cast iron its natural non-stick quality, and it builds up over time with use.

New cast iron pans usually come pre-seasoned from the factory, but that layer is thin. Building it up in the first few weeks of ownership makes a noticeable difference in how the pan performs.

To season a pan:

1. Wash it with warm water and a little mild soap (this is fine for a new pan, just not for regular cleaning later).
2. Dry it completely. Cast iron rusts fast if any moisture is left behind. A few minutes on low heat on the stove helps evaporate any remaining water.
3. Rub a very thin layer of oil (vegetable oil, canola oil, or flaxseed oil all work) over the entire surface, inside and out, including the handle.
4. Wipe off any excess. You want a thin, almost invisible layer, not a puddle. Too much oil leaves a sticky, uneven finish.
5. Bake it upside down in a 450 to 500 degree oven for about an hour, with foil on the rack below to catch drips. Let it cool in the oven.

Repeat this two or three times for a new pan, and you'll have a solid non-stick base to build on.

## Everyday Cleaning (Without Ruining the Seasoning)

The biggest myth about cast iron is that soap will ruin it. A small amount of dish soap is actually fine for regular cleaning. It's soaking it in water or scrubbing off the seasoning that causes problems.

After each use:

- Clean the pan while it's still warm, not stone cold. Food releases more easily.
- Use hot water and a stiff brush or non-metal scrubber. A little soap is okay.
- For stuck-on bits, a sprinkle of coarse salt with a small amount of oil works as a gentle abrasive.
- Dry it immediately and thoroughly, towel dry, then a minute on the stove over low heat to be sure.
- Rub on a very thin layer of oil while it's still warm to maintain the seasoning.

Avoid soaking it in the sink, putting it in the dishwasher, using steel wool or harsh metal scouring pads, and cooking highly acidic foods for long periods in a newer or under-seasoned pan.

## Storage

Store cast iron somewhere dry. If you're stacking it with other pans, put a paper towel or pan protector between them to absorb moisture and prevent scratching the seasoning. Avoid storing it with the lid on tight if there's any residual moisture, since trapped humidity is a fast track to rust.

## Common Mistakes That Ruin Cast Iron

- Leaving it wet. This is the number one cause of rust. Always dry immediately and completely.
- Skipping the oil step after cleaning. This is what maintains the seasoning long-term.
- Using it on high heat for delicate cooking. Cast iron holds heat extremely well; medium heat is usually enough, even when a recipe says high.
- Panicking over a little rust. It happens, even to careful owners. It's fixable with a scrub, a rinse, and a full re-seasoning.

## Quick FAQ

Can I use metal utensils on cast iron? Yes, cast iron is tough enough for metal utensils, unlike non-stick coatings.

Why is my food sticking? Usually means the seasoning needs building up, or the pan wasn't preheated enough before adding food.

How do I fix rust? Scrub the rusted area with steel wool until it's back to bare metal, wash, dry thoroughly, and re-season from scratch.
